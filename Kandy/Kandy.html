||Kandy - The Cultural Capital of Sri Lanka||

Tourist Places to Visit in Kandy


Kandy, nestled in the lush hills of central Sri Lanka, is a captivating city brimming with cultural richness and natural beauty. Renowned for its picturesque landscapes and historical significance, Kandy boasts several must-visit attractions. The iconic Temple of the Tooth, home to Sri Lanka's most sacred relic, draws pilgrims and tourists alike with its intricate architecture and spiritual ambiance. The Royal Botanical Gardens present a tranquil retreat, showcasing a diverse array of flora amidst stunning landscapes. Nature enthusiasts can explore the scenic Udawatta Kele Sanctuary, while history buffs can delve into the city's colonial past at the Kandy Garrison Cemetery. With its blend of spirituality, nature, and history, Kandy offers a memorable experience for visitors seeking to immerse themselves in Sri Lanka's rich tapestry of culture and heritage.

1. Temple of the Sacred Tooth Relic (Sri Dalada Maligawa)

The Temple of Tooth at kandy is the final location of the sacred tooth of the lord Buddha which was brought to srilanka during the reign of king “keerthi sri Megawarna” by prince “Dantha” and Princess “Hemamala” from the kindom of kalingu India. It became the palladium of srilankan kings and was preciously guarded in a special shrine built within precincts of royal palace where the capital was located. The Temple of the Tooth Relic of Lord Buddha, sri dalada Maligawa is in the centre of the city, the highest venerated religious place with steeped in cultural heritage attracting the highest respect of the Buddhists as well as the other religions all over the world.

The Temple of the Tooth Relic, known locally as Sri Dalada Maligawa, stands as a testament to the enduring spiritual significance of Buddhism in Sri Lanka. Beyond its historical origins, the temple has served as a focal point for religious and cultural ceremonies for centuries, embodying the essence of Sri Lankan identity and heritage. Its architectural grandeur, adorned with intricate carvings and embellishments, reflects the reverence and devotion with which the sacred tooth relic of the Buddha is revered by Buddhists worldwide. The temple complex, nestled amidst the picturesque landscape of Kandy, evokes a sense of tranquility and reverence, inviting pilgrims and visitors to experience the profound spirituality that permeates its hallowed halls. Throughout its storied history, the Temple of the Tooth Relic has endured as a symbol of faith, resilience, and unity, transcending temporal boundaries to inspire reverence and awe in the hearts of all who journey to its sacred precincts.

2. Peradeniya Botanical Gardens

The Royal Botanic Gardens, Peradeniya are located in Sri Lanka's Central Province, about 5.5 kilometers to the west of Kandy. 1.2 million residents and 400,000 tourists visited the garden in 2016.It is next to Sri Lanka's longest river, the Mahaweli.It is well known for its orchid collection. More than 4000 different plant species, including orchids, spices, mediterranean plants, and palm trees, may be found in the garden.The "National Herbarium of Sri Lanka" is connected to it. The botanical garden has a total size of 147 acres , is 460 meters above sea level, and receives 200 days' worth of rain annually. The Department of National Botanic Gardens oversees its management.

When King Wickramabahu III assumed the throne and established court in Peradeniya, close to the Mahaweli river, the Botanic Gardens first appeared. This was in 1371. Rajadhi Rajasinghe and King Kirti Sri came after that. King Wimala Dharma had a temple built here, but the British, who had taken over rule of the Kandyan Kingdom, demolished it.Then, in 1821, Alexandar Moon laid the foundation for a botanical garden. He cultivated coffee and cinnamon plants in the garden.[4] Plants from Kew Garden, Slave Island, Colombo, and the Kalutara Garden in Kalutara were used to create the Peradeniya Botanical Garden, which was officially founded in 1843. Greater independence was granted to the Royal Botanic Garden, Peradeniya.

3. Kandy Lake

Built in 1807 by King Sri Wickrama Rajasinghe near to the Temple of the Tooth, Kandy Lake , also known as Kiri Muhuda or the Sea of Milk, is an artificial lake in the center of the hill city of Kandy, Sri Lanka. Its size was diminished over time. Fishing is prohibited since the lake is protected. Kandy Lake covers an area of 6,544 square meters. The radius is 3.21 kilometers. 18.5 meters is the maximum depth. The parapet wall, known as Walakulu Bemma, is 633.82 meters long and has the appearance of a cloud. In the past, the structure at the lake's center, together with several antiquated ruins, was referred to as Diyatilaka Mandapaya. This pavilion is thought to have been utilized by the Kings for leisure.There are places to walk or run around Kandy Lake. Views of the hills and the town are available from the trail that circles the lake in the shade. Atop a hill overlooking the lake sits the Malwatte temple, one of the two main temples of the Theravada Buddhist Siyam Nikaya sect.


Tigolwela, a section of paddy fields, was once the lake in front of the Temple of the Tooth. King Sri Wickrama Rajasinha transformed it into a lake in 1807. The lake built afterwards was also called Kiri-muhuda since there had previously been a pond with the name Kiri-muhuda in the center of the Tigolwela. Kandy Lake is credited to architect Deveda Moolacharya. The steps leading into the lake by the Mahamaluwa are still discernible on the Paththirippuwa (octagon) side, where the monarch initially constructed a dam across the paddy fields, extending over to the Poya-maluwa. The dam, on which a road was built, made it possible for the king to cross over to the Malwatte Vihare.

4. Hanthana Mountain Range

The Hanthana Mountain Range, is a verdant paradise brimming with natural beauty and ecological diversity. Rising majestically to heights of around 3800 feet above sea level, the Hanthana Range is a beloved destination for hikers, nature enthusiasts, and adventure seekers alike. Its lush forests, cascading waterfalls, and panoramic viewpoints offer a scenic retreat from the bustling city below, providing a sanctuary for both wildlife and weary travelers.

The Hanthana Range is not only a haven for biodiversity but also a vital watershed for the region, feeding numerous streams and rivers that sustain the surrounding ecosystems and communities. The mountain range is home to a rich variety of flora and fauna, including endemic species such as the Hanthana Montane Hourglass Frog and the Hanthana Slender Loris, making it a hotspot for conservation efforts and scientific research. Visitors to the Hanthana Mountains can explore its network of hiking trails, ranging from leisurely strolls to challenging treks, each offering unique perspectives of the region's natural splendor.


Beyond its ecological significance, the Hanthana Mountain Range holds cultural and historical significance for the people of Sri Lanka. It has served as a backdrop for countless legends and folklore, woven into the fabric of local traditions and heritage. The ancient ruins scattered throughout the mountains bear testament to centuries of human habitation and spiritual reverence, offering glimpses into the storied past of this mystical landscape. As a symbol of resilience and natural beauty, the Hanthana Range continues to captivate the hearts and minds of all who venture into its emerald embrace, leaving an indelible mark on those who seek solace and adventure amidst its timeless allure.

5. Embekke Devalaya

In Sri Lanka, during the Gampola Era , King Vikramabahu III constructed the Embekka Devalaya (Embekka Temple). The Kataragama deviyo is the focus of the Devalaya. At this location, people also worship Devatha Bandara, a local god. The "Sanctum of Garagha", the "Digge" or "Dancing Hall," and the "Hevisi Mandapaya" or "Drummers' Hall" are the three sections that make up the shrine. Visitors have gravitated toward the Drummers' Hall because of the exquisite wood carvings on its elaborate pillars and its steeply pitched roof.

Some of the timber used for the "Drummers' Hall" is reported to have come from a Gampola "Royal Audience Hall" that had been abandoned. The hall may have undergone restorations while the Sinhalese Kings of Kandy were in power. Some of the best specimens of Sinhalese art can be found on the carvings that grace the wooden pillars of the drummers' hall and the "Vahalkada" (the Devala's entrance porch, which is thought to be older). The wooden pillars have an octagonal base and a square-shaped "Pekada" at the top made of four leaves. The carvings of interwoven swans, double -headed eagles, entwined rope patterns, nursing mothers, and soldiers fighting on are among the most notable and well -known.

6. Udawattakele Reserve Forest

The Udawattakele Reserve Forest, nestled adjacent to the Temple of the Tooth in Kandy, Sri Lanka, is a pristine sanctuary of biodiversity and tranquility. Spread across approximately 257 acres, this lush forest reserve offers a verdant retreat from the hustle and bustle of urban life. Home to a diverse array of flora and fauna, including towering trees, exotic orchids, and elusive wildlife such as monkeys and endemic bird species, Udawattakele captivates visitors with its natural splendor and ecological richness.

Beyond its ecological significance, Udawattakele holds historical and cultural importance, with ancient pathways and ruins dating back to the Kandyan Kingdom. The forest served as a royal reserve for the Kandyan monarchs, who sought solace and serenity amidst its verdant landscapes. Today, visitors can explore its winding trails and hidden glades, discovering ancient cave dwellings, meditation sites, and picturesque viewpoints that offer panoramic vistas of Kandy and its surrounding hills. As a haven for nature lovers and history enthusiasts alike, Udawattakele Reserve Forest beckons travelers to immerse themselves in its timeless beauty and uncover the secrets of Sri Lanka's rich natural and cultural heritage.

7. Kandy National Museum

The Kandy National Museum stands as a custodian of Sri Lanka's rich cultural heritage, offering visitors a captivating journey through the island's storied past. Housed within the historic Palle Vahala building, which once served as the royal palace of King Sri Wickrama Rajasinghe, the museum is a treasure trove of artifacts, relics, and exhibits spanning centuries of Sri Lankan history and civilization. From intricate woodcarvings and traditional costumes to ancient weaponry and religious artifacts, the museum's collections provide invaluable insights into the island's diverse cultural tapestry and the legacies of its dynasties and kingdoms.

As visitors wander through the museum's halls, they encounter a vivid tableau of Sri Lanka's rich artistic traditions, architectural marvels, and archaeological wonders. Exhibits showcase the island's vibrant folk traditions, religious practices, and historical milestones, offering a comprehensive overview of its cultural evolution and enduring legacy. The Kandy National Museum serves not only as a repository of national treasures but also as a beacon of education, enlightenment, and cultural pride, inviting visitors to embark on a captivating voyage of discovery and appreciation for the timeless wonders of Sri Lanka's cultural heritage.

8. The Ceylon Tea Museum

The Ceylon Tea Museum, nestled in the heart of Sri Lanka's verdant hill country in Kandy, offers visitors a fascinating glimpse into the island's rich tea heritage. Housed within the historic Hanthana Tea Factory, the museum showcases the evolution of Sri Lanka's tea industry, from its humble beginnings to its status as one of the world's leading tea producers. Exhibits at the museum highlight the intricate process of tea cultivation, harvesting, and production, providing insights into the craftsmanship and expertise that have made Ceylon tea renowned worldwide for its exceptional quality and flavor.

As visitors explore the museum's galleries, they are treated to a sensory journey through the sights, sounds, and aromas of Sri Lanka's tea country. From vintage tea-processing machinery to interactive displays detailing the art of tea tasting and blending, the Ceylon Tea Museum offers a captivating immersion into the traditions, innovations, and cultural significance of tea in Sri Lankan society. With its picturesque setting amidst lush tea plantations and panoramic views of the surrounding hills, the museum provides a memorable experience for tea enthusiasts and travelers eager to unravel the secrets behind the world's favorite brew.
